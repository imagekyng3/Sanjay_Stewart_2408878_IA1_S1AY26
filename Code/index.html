AI Business Idea

AI for Environmental Cleanup

I've been thinking about how AI could help tackle Jamaica's litter problem. My idea is an app that uses image recognition to identify and log trash in real-time. Users could take photos of litter, the app maps it, and local communities get notified about cleanup needs in their area.

How it would work here: In Kingston, we have serious issues with blocked drains and pollution. This app could reward people (maybe through partnerships with local businesses) for reporting and cleaning up litter. Schools could use it for community service projects. It turns a frustrating problem into something people can actually solve together.

Why it matters: Jamaica loses tourism revenue and deals with flooding partly because of poor waste management. An app like this could change behavior by making cleanup visible and rewarding.

Ethical concerns: Privacy is a big one—photos might accidentally capture people's faces or homes. There's also the risk of the reward system being gamed (people littering just to clean it up for points). And not everyone has a smartphone with good camera and data access.

My solutions: Auto-blur faces and license plates in photos. Cap daily rewards per user to prevent gaming. Make the app work offline with sync later, and partner with community centers where people can use shared devices to participate.

Real example: Litterati does something similar. They crowdsource litter data globally. Check them out: litterati.org

Personal Development Reflections

Twelve Pillars by Jim Rohn - Chapters 4-7

Chapter 4: Achieve Your Goals

This chapter hit me because I've always had vague ideas about what I wanted, but never wrote them down properly. Rohn says dreams without goals are just wishes. He's right. When I started writing specific goals with deadlines—like "finish this web dev assignment by Friday" instead of "get better at coding"—I actually started making progress I could measure.

I'm applying this by keeping a goals journal. Every Sunday I write three specific things I want to accomplish that week. It's simple but it works because there's no confusion about what I'm aiming for.

Chapter 5: Use Time Wisely

The time chapter made me realize how much I waste. Rohn talks about how successful people guard their time like it's gold. I used to scroll through social media for hours without even noticing. Now I'm more intentional. I block out study time, set timers for breaks, and actually plan my day the night before.

The biggest change? Mornings. I wake up knowing exactly what I need to do instead of figuring it out as I go. That one shift saved me probably two hours a day that used to disappear into random browsing.

Chapter 6: Surround Yourself with the Best People

This one was uncomfortable because it made me evaluate my friendships. Rohn says you become the average of the five people you spend the most time with. I looked around and realized some people in my circle weren't going anywhere, and worse, they didn't want me to either.

I didn't cut anyone off harshly, but I did start spending more time with classmates who actually care about their grades and future. I joined study groups. I started asking my professors questions after class. The shift in my mindset has been noticeable—I think bigger now because I'm around people who think bigger.

Chapter 7: Be a Lifelong Learner

Rohn argues that formal education makes you a living, but self-education makes you a fortune. Tech changes so fast that if I only relied on what's taught in class, I'd already be behind. I started watching YouTube tutorials, reading documentation, and following developers on GitHub to see how they solve problems.

I also started reading more—not just tech books but books about business, psychology, and personal development. The more I learn outside my major, the more connections I see. It all feeds into making me better at what I do.
